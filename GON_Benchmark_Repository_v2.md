## THREE-TEST RULE (HARD RULE - March 29, 2026)

Every benchmark must pass all three simultaneously:
1. **RECENCY** - within 24 months, or explicitly flagged as best available
2. **SPECIFICITY** - applies to the exact market and sector discussed, not a regional average
3. **OPERATOR INTERPRETABILITY** - JP can explain what the number means for someone deploying capital

- All 3 pass = publish with confidence
- 2 of 3 = publish with named caveat
- 1 or fewer = directional context only, never a benchmark

**Next review date for all entries: September 2026** (6-month cycle)

### BENCHMARK D-001
**Metric:** Mobile money agent share of commission pool
**Market:** Cameroon (CEMAC)
**Sector:** Fintech / Mobile money
**Value:** Agents receive 40-45% of the commission pool. Remaining 55-60% shared among the mobile network operator (MTN or Orange), partner banks, and the superagent manager.
**Recency:** June 2024
**Source:** ICTD Working Paper 192 - "Cameroon's Tax on Mobile Money: Implications for Agents' Performance and Revenue Sustainability"
**Authors:** Alphonse Noah and Ruth Tacneng
**URL:** https://opendocs.ids.ac.uk/opendocs/bitstream/handle/20.500.12413/18369/ICTD_WP192_FINAL.pdf
**Methodology:** Survey of 807 active mobile money agents in Cameroon's Centre Region (includes Yaounde). MTN and Orange markets. 15 months post-tax-introduction.
**THREE-TEST STATUS:** PASSES ALL THREE
**Next review:** June 2026
**Notes:** Cameroon is CEMAC not WAEMU. BCEAO data (see D-003) suggests 45-55% agent share of gross transaction revenue across WAEMU - broadly consistent. Use D-001 for CEMAC, D-003 for WAEMU.

### BENCHMARK R-001
**Metric:** BCEAO e-money issuer regulation - current WAEMU framework
**Market:** WAEMU (Ivory Coast, Senegal, Mali, Burkina Faso, Benin, Togo, Niger, Guinea-Bissau)
**Sector:** Fintech / Mobile money / Insurtech
**Instrument:** Instruction n°001/05/2021, consolidated by Instruction n°001/01/2024
**Date:** May 31, 2021 (original); 2024 (consolidated)
**Key provisions:**

**Source:** BCEAO official instruments
**URL:** bceao.int/instruction-001-05-2021
**THREE-TEST STATUS:** PASSES ALL THREE
**Next review:** September 2026
**IC Checklist implication:** A founder in a WAEMU market claiming contractual exclusivity with a mobile money operator may be in conflict with Article 18. The WAEMU exclusivity flag in IC Checklist v3.1 is grounded in this instrument.

### BENCHMARK R-004
**Metric:** CEMAC primary payment services regulation
**Market:** CEMAC (Cameroon, Congo, Gabon, Chad, CAR, Equatorial Guinea)
**Sector:** Fintech / Mobile money / Payments
**Instrument:** Règlement n°04/18/CEMAC/UMAC/COBAC relatif aux services de paiement dans la CEMAC
**Issuing body:** UMAC (Comité Ministériel de l'Union Monétaire de l'Afrique Centrale)
**Date:** December 21, 2018
**Key provisions:**
- Decoupled payment services from pure banking, creating two new entity types: Etablissement de Paiement (EP) and Etablissement de Monnaie Electronique (EME)
- Foundational instrument for all mobile money and fintech regulation in CEMAC - equivalent to BCEAO's pre-2021 framework
**URL:** beac.int/reglement-04-18-services-de-paiement
**THREE-TEST STATUS:** FAILS RECENCY (2018 - outside 24 months, but foundational - best available), PASSES SPECIFICITY, PASSES INTERPRETABILITY
**Next review:** September 2026
**Notes:** The 2018 regulation remains operative. Supplemented by BEAC Instruction n°001/GR/2024 (see R-005). No equivalent to BCEAO's 2024 anti-exclusivity provision exists in CEMAC - exclusivity is challenged case-by-case under general competition law.

### BENCHMARK R-007
**Metric:** COBAC capital requirements for payment and e-money institutions - Cameroon 2026
**Market:** Cameroon (CEMAC)
**Sector:** Fintech / Payments / E-money
**Instrument:** COBAC Regulation R-2025/01 (Articles 45-50 of Reglement n°04/18 for EP)
**Date:** Effective January 1, 2026
**Value:**
- Etablissement de Paiement (EP): 500 million FCFA minimum capital (approx. $830k). Agrément issued by Ministry of Finance with COBAC opinion. Timeline: 6-12 months.
- Etablissement de Monnaie Electronique (EME): 4 billion FCFA minimum capital (approx. $6.6M). Agrément issued by Ministry of Finance with COBAC opinion. Timeline: 9-15 months.
**THREE-TEST STATUS:** PASSES ALL THREE
**Next review:** January 2027
**IC Checklist implication:** The EME capital requirement in Cameroon ($6.6M) is 13x the equivalent BCEAO requirement in WAEMU ($495k). For any Series A startup in Cameroon wanting to issue its own e-money wallets, this threshold makes the partner model (operating under an existing bank or MNO licence) the only viable path. Flag this structural constraint in regulatory dimension for all Cameroon EME-dependent deals.

### BENCHMARK R-008
**Metric:** Kenya non-deposit taking credit provider regulation 2025
**Market:** Kenya
**Sector:** Fintech / Digital lending
**Instrument:** Central Bank of Kenya (Non-Deposit Taking Credit Providers) Regulations, 2025
**Issuing body:** Central Bank of Kenya (CBK)
**Date:** Drafted August 2025; implementation ongoing through 2026
**Key provisions:**
- Replaces "Digital Credit Provider" terminology with Non-Deposit Taking Credit Provider (NDTCP) - expands scope to include offline lenders
- Tiered licensing: entities with capital above KES 20 million (approx. $155k) require full licence; below must register
- CBK now has explicit authority to pre-approve all interest rates and fees - direct price controls
- Annual licence fee: KES 500,000
**URL:** centralbank.go.ke/draft-ndtcp-regulations-2025
**THREE-TEST STATUS:** PASSES ALL THREE
**Next review:** September 2026
**IC Checklist implication:** Any Kenya digital lending deal must now account for CBK price controls on interest rates. A lending model built on interest rate assumptions that require CBK pre-approval faces a structural regulatory dependency that is not a founder-controlled variable.

**Metric:** Tanzania mobile money and payments regulatory framework 2024-2025

### BENCHMARK R-010
**Metric:** Rwanda fintech regulatory sandbox and Kenya-Rwanda passporting agreement
**Market:** Rwanda / Kenya
**Sector:** Fintech / Payments / Cross-border
**Instrument:** Regulation No 41/2022 (Regulatory Sandbox); Directive No 4230/2025-00042 on foreign currency transactions; Kenya-Rwanda Fintech License Passporting Agreement
**Issuing body:** National Bank of Rwanda (BNR)
**Date:** 2022 (sandbox); September 2025 (FX directive); March 2026 (passporting)
**Key provisions:**
- Rwanda regulatory sandbox allows fintech testing with reduced compliance burden before full licensing
- March 2026: Kenya and Rwanda signed a fintech licence passporting agreement - a payment provider licensed by BNR can operate in Kenya (and vice versa) without obtaining a second licence, subject to joint technical committee standards
- Rwanda PSP minimum capital: RWF 100M to 500M (approx. $75k to $380k) - significantly lower than Kenya's KES 20M threshold for full licence
**URL:** bnr.rw/regulatory-sandbox
**THREE-TEST STATUS:** PASSES ALL THREE
**Next review:** September 2026
**IC Checklist implication:** Rwanda is now the most capital-efficient entry point into the East African corridor. A startup can licence in Rwanda at lower capital cost and passport into the Kenyan consumer market. For deals targeting East Africa, the Rwanda-first entry strategy materially changes the capitalisation and regulatory timeline assumptions. This is the single most actionable regulatory finding from the March 2026 prompt round.
