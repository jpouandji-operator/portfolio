BCEAO Just Ended Wave's Moat. Nobody Has Priced This Yet.

Two separate BCEAO interventions - one banning exclusivity in payment partnerships, one forcing settlement-layer interoperability - are compressing the structural moat that let WAEMU's mobile money leaders operate as closed loops. The compliance deadline lands in September 2026. Most diligence memos I have seen do not model it.

Most of the coverage I have seen on BCEAO's new instant payment platform treats it as a plumbing upgrade - a regional interoperability rail, filed under infrastructure modernization. That framing misses what is actually happening. Read the two BCEAO interventions together, on their actual timeline, and this is not plumbing. It is a moat-removal event, and it is landing on a fixed compliance date most Africa fintech underwriting has not touched.

Start with the part that already happened. BCEAO's Instruction n°001-01-2024, issued 23 January 2024, rewrote the licensing framework for payment services across WAEMU and, critically, prohibited exclusivity clauses in payment service providers' partnership agreements with third parties. That single provision matters more than its dry legal language suggests: exclusivity in agent networks and distribution partnerships was one of the two real moats a mobile money leader could build in this region. As of May 2025, the licensing transition period closed - operating without a license is no longer optional.

The Second Moat Is Going Now, Not Later

The second intervention is the one nobody has priced in, because it is still unfolding. BCEAO built PI-SPI - the region's interoperable instant payment platform - piloting it from July 2024, moving to real-condition testing in June 2025, and formally launching it on 30 September 2025. By October 2025, reporting on early adoption showed a lopsided picture: three banks accounted for the bulk of platform activity, and Wave's absence was conspicuous enough to be the headline. Then, in April 2026, BCEAO issued a hard mandate at the fintechs and telcos it judged to be stalling integration or working around the rules through commercial loopholes. The result is a firm deadline - banks, e-money institutions and payment institutions must be connected by 30 September 2026, with microfinance institutions given until June 2027.

Put the two interventions together and the pattern is not "more regulation." It is the systematic removal of both moats a WAEMU mobile money business could hold. Exclusivity in partnership contracts was moat one, and the 2024 instruction closed it. Closed-loop settlement - the fact that money moving between two Wave wallets is instant and free precisely because it never leaves Wave's own ledger - was moat two. PI-SPI closes that one, by making a Wave wallet, an Orange Money wallet and a bank account fungible for instant transfer purposes at the settlement layer. Once that happens, the thing a leader was actually selling - "we are the fastest, cheapest way to move money in this market" - stops being proprietary. It becomes a shared utility.

This is not a prediction that any specific operator collapses. Distribution depth, agent density and brand trust do not disappear on a regulatory deadline. But the specific and most defensible part of the story - the network-effect argument that justified premium multiples - is exactly what this pair of interventions targets. That is a distinction most surface-level coverage of "Africa fintech regulation" does not make, because it requires having operated inside a distribution stack where exclusivity and closed-loop settlement were the entire commercial logic, not abstract regulatory line items.

Where This Hits the IC Checklist

Run this through the checklist's regulatory risk dimension and the question changes shape. It is no longer "does this market have a stable regulator" - WAEMU's does, and BCEAO has been consistent and transparent about its timeline. It is "what percentage of this company's stated moat is a closed-loop settlement advantage that a named regulatory deadline removes in Q3 2026, and was that modeled in the underwriting?" On distribution economics, the same interoperability that strips Wave's moat also strips the telco incumbents' fee-based defense against it - everyone's pricing power compresses at once, which is a different competitive dynamic than "challenger disrupts incumbent." And on exit pathway, an acquirer nine months from now is not buying a proprietary network effect. They are buying agent reach, license position and off-ramp infrastructure - which changes what the right comps for a WAEMU payments exit actually are, closer to the infrastructure logic I laid out when MTN absorbed IHS Towers than to a typical fintech multiple.

The underwriting question: For any WAEMU mobile money position in your portfolio, what share of the stated moat depends on closed-loop settlement rather than agent density, license position, or brand trust - and does that share survive 30 September 2026, the date PI-SPI integration stops being optional? If your last diligence memo does not answer that with a number, it has not actually priced this yet.

The regulatory story runs in parallel to a broader pattern across WAEMU fintech that I have tracked in the stablecoin infrastructure piece - regulators consistently reward whoever owns the compliant plumbing, not whoever owns the flashiest front end. BCEAO is simply running that same logic one layer up, at the interoperability rail itself, and doing it on a public calendar.
