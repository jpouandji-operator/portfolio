The Africa IC Checklist

A pre-IC pressure test on Africa-specific deal assumptions. Six operator-sourced dimensions. Output: the questions to ask before you wire.

GON is the operator intelligence layer for African private capital - built from twelve years inside the distribution stack, maintained against primary institutional sources, and validated against real deal outcomes.

What this is

This is not a scoring tool. It is a structured challenge built on twelve years of operating digital products across African markets. It surfaces the assumptions that survive every pitch meeting because they live below the line of the financial model - in the distribution cost footnote, in the regulatory risk paragraph, in the team slide that lists years of experience without naming a single crisis navigated.

It works by forcing specific inputs. Vague answers trigger gates that tell you exactly what you do not yet know. The memo output gives you questions, not verdicts. A deal that clears all six dimensions is not approved - it is adequately stress-tested on these six. Four dimensions remain uncovered and are flagged dynamically in your output.

If you are filling this in to look good, you are wasting your time. The tool rewards honesty and penalises polish.

This is a living tool. It improves with every case it is run on. The Copia Global war games retrospective (2026) flagged two gaps that are now in v3.2. If this tool misses something on your deal, the feedback form at the end feeds directly into the next version. Operator intelligence compounds through use.
Assessment is strongest in Francophone West Africa and CEMAC markets, where the operator benchmarks are grounded in direct experience. East Africa dimensions are benchmarked against primary institutional sources - CBK, Bank of Tanzania, BNR, GSMA - but not operator-validated at the same depth. This boundary is named because intellectual honesty about where the data is strong and where it is thinner is itself an operator signal.
Worked example - Series A fintech, Ivory Coast, mobile money payments
Same deal. Same market. Same stage. Two founders. One has done the work. One has learned the vocabulary.
Founder who has done the work
Gross margin: 22%
Stack breakdown: Yes - agent 40% of pool, Orange Money 35%, superagent 15%, net to us 10%
Distribution moat: Semi-exclusive - preferred status with 3 superagents, not contractual
Retention: 34% of paying customers returned within 60 days without a promotion
Regulatory risk: BCEAO e-money licence revision - we depend on Orange Money's licence; if they charge higher revenue share we lose 8% margin overnight. Plan B: direct BCEAO licence application in progress.
This produces a specific, pressured memo. The tool flags the semi-exclusive distribution as amber. It flags the 34% retention as the number to defend. The regulatory scenario gives the IC three specific questions to ask.
Founder who has learned the vocabulary
Gross margin: 65%
Stack breakdown: Yes - at a high level
Distribution moat: Strong agent network across Abidjan
Retention: Strong repeat usage confirmed
Regulatory risk: We monitor the regulatory environment closely and have good relationships with the relevant authorities.
This triggers multiple gates. 65% gross margin for a mobile money agent model is implausible. "High level" stack trips the insufficient gate. Vague retention has no number to defend. The regulatory answer is a disclaimer, not a scenario. The memo flags all four as red.

Dimension 1 of 6
Operator lens
Distribution economics and moat
"The cost of the distribution model is one question. Whether that model is defensible is a different and more important question. In African markets a well-capitalised competitor can replicate an agent network in six months by paying higher commissions. The moat is not the channel - it is what makes the channel exclusive."
Founder's stated gross margin *
Enter as stated - e.g. "35%" or "35-40%". Do not adjust for what you think it should be.
Distribution model claimed
Select the model as described in the pitch materials
Has the founder broken down the full margin stack - every intermediary layer, every fee?
A mobile money model with "35% gross margin" but no stack breakdown is unverifiable. Each intermediary layer must be named and costed.
Is the distribution channel exclusive, or can a competitor replicate it by paying more?
Preferred status is not the same as contractual exclusivity. The distinction matters at IC.
Regulatory conflict flag: BCEAO Instruction n°001/01/2024 Article 18 formally prohibits exclusivity clauses in telco-fintech partnerships across all WAEMU markets. A founder claiming contractual exclusivity with a telco in Ivory Coast, Senegal, or another WAEMU market may be describing a clause that is unenforceable or in conflict with current regulation. Verify the contractual basis and whether it has been reviewed against this instrument before IC.
Insufficient information on distribution economics. A single gross margin number is not a distribution model. Do not proceed to IC without the full stack broken down by layer and a clear answer on channel exclusivity.
Dimension 2 of 6
Operator lens
Regulatory timing and incumbent capture
"The regulatory risk most founders describe is government action against them. The risk most investors miss is regulatory capture by an incumbent - a telco or bank with regulator relationships using licensing conditions or delays to disadvantage a competitor. In Francophone West Africa, this pattern is more common than direct government intervention."
Regulatory dependency *
Depends on a partner's licence is the highest-risk dependency - if the partner loses or changes their licence, the business model breaks.
Has the founder named a specific regulatory risk scenario?
General regulatory awareness is not a specific scenario. The question is: which exact decision, on what timeline, and what happens to the business model the week after it is announced.
Is there a dominant incumbent in this market that benefits from the founder failing - and do they have regulatory access?
In Francophone West Africa: Orange and MTN have longstanding central bank relationships. This is the most common undisclosed risk in fintech due diligence.
Contingency model if primary regulatory assumption breaks
If the founder has not modelled this, ask them to. The answer reveals how seriously they have stress-tested the operating environment.
Insufficient information on regulatory exposure. A founder who cannot name the specific decision that breaks their model - and has not considered incumbent capture risk - has not stress-tested the operating environment. Flag before IC.
Dimension 3 of 6
Structural
Exit pathway realism and acquirability
"The exit question has two parts. Who would buy it - and is it buyable? In African markets, the gap between a growing company and an acquirable company is large. Informal governance, unclear cap tables, and related-party structures that would fail a multinational's due diligence are common at early stages and rarely cleaned up before they become a deal-breaker."
Stated exit route *
Africa's exit-to-investment ratio improved to 0.2x in 2025. Trade sales remain 38% of exits. An undiscussed exit at Series A or above is a material gap.
Has the founder named specific acquirers with rationale?
A cold name on a slide is not the same as a warm relationship with someone who owns the M&A decision at that organisation.
Is the business acquirable - clean governance, auditable financials, no problematic cap table structures?
A growing business is not the same as an acquirable business. Related-party structures that seem minor at Series A become deal-breakers for multinational acquirers.
Insufficient information on exit realism. Africa's exit-to-investment ratio improved to 0.2x in 2025 but secondaries now represent 26% of exits. An exit thesis without named acquirers, a clear rationale, and basic acquirability confirmed is not a thesis - it is a hope. Source: AVCA 2025 Activity Report.
Dimension 4 of 6
Operator lens
Demand validation and recurrence
"Demographics are not demand. And first-time paying customers are not recurring revenue. The question that predicts whether unit economics hold at scale is not whether someone paid once. It is whether they came back without a promotion or a founder intervention."
Primary demand evidence *
Select the strongest form of evidence available in the materials. Do not upgrade vague statements - if retention data was not shown, select accordingly.
Is the demand recurring or episodic?
Promotion-driven repeat usage is not the same as organic recurrence. M-Pesa Kenya benchmark: 37.92 transactions per active customer per month without promotions. That is the bar.
What existing behaviour does this replace and what does the customer currently pay for it?
If the product replaces a behaviour with no known current cost, the willingness-to-pay assumption is unanchored. New behaviour creation is the highest-risk demand thesis.
Demand based on demographics or surveys alone is insufficient. A 2024 peer-reviewed systematic review found face-to-face WTP surveys overstate actual willingness to pay by 23-29% in collectivist cultures. Enrollment gaps of 30-50 percentage points between stated willingness and actual uptake are documented across African market studies. Source: Bayked et al., Frontiers in Public Health, April 2024.
Dimension 5 of 6
Operator lens
Team resilience and network depth
"Experience predicts understanding. Network predicts survival. The three moments that kill African market companies - regulatory intervention, banking relationship crisis, key distributor defection - all require phone numbers, not just experience. A founder who spent five years at a multinational in Lagos has the experience but often not the network that matters."
In-market operating experience *
Ivory Coast and Senegal are different operating environments despite sharing a currency. Nigeria and Ghana share a language and little else. Regional experience is not country-specific experience.
Has the team navigated a material operational crisis in a comparable market?
A specific crisis with a specific resolution is evidence of operational resilience. General market challenges are not a crisis.
Does the team have direct relationships with the entities that matter in a crisis - regulators, banking partners, key distribution channel owners?
Named relationships means: a specific person at the regulator who knows the founder, a banking partner contact who picks up the phone at 6am. General market contacts do not qualify.
Team resilience is under-assessed. The three crisis moments that kill African market companies - regulatory intervention, banking relationship failure, key distributor defection - all require specific network relationships, not just experience. A team with no named relationships with regulators, banking partners, or channel owners is structurally exposed. Flag before IC.
Dimension 6 of 6
Structural
Currency and FX risk
"For a USD or EUR fund investing in local-currency revenue businesses, the return is a function of the exit multiple and the currency trajectory. A business that grows 40% in NGN terms can return nothing in USD terms if the naira depreciates 50% over the hold period. Nigeria 2023 is not a hypothetical."
Revenue and cost currency structure *
Imported goods priced in USD with local currency revenue creates a natural FX squeeze when the local currency weakens - exactly what happened to several Kenya e-commerce models in 2023.
Target market currency regime
CFA franc peg to EUR reduces volatility risk but does not eliminate repatriation cost. NGN lost approximately 70% against USD between 2022 and 2024.
If the fund is USD/EUR denominated, does the founder have a repatriation and FX hedging plan?
A founder who has not modelled repatriation for a USD/EUR fund investing in NGN or ETB revenue is leaving the primary return risk unaddressed.
Currency risk is unassessed. For USD/EUR funds investing in local-currency revenue businesses in high-volatility markets, FX exposure is often the primary return risk. The NGN lost approximately 70% of its value against USD between 2022 and 2024. A repatriation plan and FX strategy are not optional for deals in volatile currency markets.
