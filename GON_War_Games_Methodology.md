# GON WAR GAMES RETROSPECTIVES - METHODOLOGY
**Created:** March 31, 2026
**Owner:** Jean-Philippe Ouandji - Growth Operator Notes
**Purpose:** Defines the methodology for running IC Checklist retrospectives on known Africa deals. Must be read and understood before the first retrospective publishes. This document is itself publishable - it signals intellectual honesty about how the assessments work.

---

## WHAT A WAR GAMES RETROSPECTIVE IS

A war games retrospective runs the Africa IC Checklist on a real, completed deal using only information that was publicly available at the time of the investment decision - not information that emerged afterward.

The output is not a verdict on the deal or the investors. It is a demonstration of what the operator intelligence layer would have surfaced at the pre-IC stage, compared to what actually happened. Where the tool flags correctly, it validates the assessment framework. Where the tool misses something or flags a false positive, it improves the framework.

The format serves three audiences simultaneously:
- **VCs** - demonstrates the tool's practical value on deals they recognise
- **JP's professional positioning** - shows operator-level analytical depth on named deals
- **The GON model** - each retrospective is a calibration event that improves the benchmark repository and IC Checklist

---

## THE CORE METHODOLOGY PRINCIPLE

**The hardline rule: only information available at the time of investment is permitted in the assessment.**

This means:

- Press coverage, investor announcements, and founder interviews published before the investment date: permitted
- Regulatory filings, annual reports, and market studies published before the investment date: permitted
- Post-investment founder reflections, post-mortem analyses, and outcome reporting: NOT permitted in the assessment section
- Hindsight benchmarks - data we now have that did not exist at investment time: NOT permitted in the assessment section

The outcome section of each retrospective is separate from the assessment section. The outcome is described factually after the assessment is complete. The assessment never references the outcome.

This principle must be stated explicitly in every published retrospective. It is not a footnote - it is the credibility mechanism.

## THE FIVE-PART RETROSPECTIVE STRUCTURE

### Part 1: Deal Context (what was publicly known)
- Company name, sector, market
- Investment stage and approximate ticket (if disclosed)
- Lead investors
- Date of investment round
- What the company claimed to be doing at the time - sourced from press coverage and founder statements available before the investment date
- What the market context was at the time - regulatory environment, competitive landscape, macro conditions

### Part 2: IC Checklist Inputs (reconstructed from public information)
- Run each of the six IC Checklist dimensions using only pre-investment information
- For each dimension: state what was publicly knowable, what was not publicly knowable, and what input this produces in the tool
- Where information was not publicly available at the time: state this explicitly and flag the dimension as "insufficient public information" rather than guessing
- The worked inputs are shown transparently - readers can see exactly what went into each dimension

### Part 3: What the Tool Would Have Flagged
- The memo output as it would have appeared with the reconstructed inputs
- Red flags, amber flags, and green dimensions shown exactly as the tool produces them
- IC questions the tool would have generated for each dimension
- Operator benchmark text shown for each flagged dimension

### Part 4: What Actually Happened
- Factual account of the company's trajectory after investment
- Sources: published press coverage, company announcements, investor statements
- No editorialising on investor decisions - the facts are presented, not judged
- Where the tool flagged correctly: noted
- Where the tool missed something material: noted honestly
- Where the tool flagged a false positive: noted honestly

### Part 5: What This Teaches the Model
- Specific benchmark repository entries that this retrospective validates, challenges, or adds
- Specific IC Checklist dimensions or questions that should be updated based on this retrospective
- Any new failure modes or structural patterns surfaced by this case
- Honest acknowledgment of what the tool cannot detect - informal economy dynamics, founder motivation, political economy at the deal level

## CANDIDATE DEAL SELECTION CRITERIA

A deal is suitable for a war games retrospective if it meets ALL of the following:

1. **Outcome is documented** - the deal has a clear, publicly known outcome (shutdown, pivot, acquisition, continued operation with disclosed trajectory)
2. **Pre-investment information is accessible** - sufficient press coverage, investor announcements, and market context existed at the time to reconstruct the IC Checklist inputs
3. **At least two years have passed** since the investment date - sufficient time for the outcome to be clear and for the investor sensitivity to be lower
4. **The failure modes are traceable** - the factors that drove the outcome are publicly documented or can be reasonably inferred from public sources
5. **No active litigation** - the company or its investors are not in active legal proceedings that could be affected by public analysis

## HANDLING INVESTOR SENSITIVITY

Africa's VC community is small. The same investors appear across deals and are potential GON readers and collaborators. The retrospective format must be designed to maintain those relationships, not damage them.

**Three principles:**

**Factual, not judgmental.** The retrospective describes what happened and what the tool flags. It does not say "the investors made a mistake" or "this was a bad deal." Investment decisions involve information the IC Checklist cannot access - fund strategy, portfolio construction logic, LP commitments, proprietary diligence. The tool's flags are operator-layer observations, not investment verdicts.

**Honest about information gaps.** Where the IC Checklist would have had insufficient information to assess a dimension, this is stated explicitly. "Insufficient public information at the time" is a legitimate and honest assessment. It signals rigour, not weakness.

**Empathetic about difficulty.** Africa deals are hard. The structural challenges the IC Checklist flags - distribution margin compression, regulatory volatility, demand validation gaps - are not founder or investor failures. They are the market conditions that make Africa investing genuinely difficult. The retrospective framing should reflect that.

**What to avoid:**
- Named attribution of investor error
- Speculation about private information or internal decisions
- Language that reads as "I would have known better"
- Comparison of investor track records

## FIRST RETROSPECTIVE: COPIA GLOBAL

**Why Copia:**
- Shut down July 2024 - outcome is unambiguous and documented
- Well covered in press (TechCrunch, TechCabal, Quartz Africa) with timeline going back to Series B
- Covers three IC Checklist dimensions simultaneously: distribution economics, demand validation, unit economics trajectory
- Investors include Flourish Ventures, Endeavor Catalyst, 4DX Ventures - all still active, all respected in the ecosystem
- JP has no prior relationship with the company or its investors - no conflict of interest

**Pre-investment press coverage to source:**
- Series B announcement ($30M, 2021) - TechCrunch coverage
- Copia founder interviews 2019-2021 on distribution model and demand thesis
- Kenya e-commerce and last-mile logistics market context 2019-2021
- Comparable company coverage (Jumia Kenya, Copia competitor landscape)

**IC Checklist dimensions most likely to generate flags:**
- Distribution economics - last-mile logistics ownership, agent network unit economics
- Demand validation - rural Kenya e-commerce demand evidence at the time
- Unit economics trajectory - not covered in current tool but flagged in missing dimensions panel

**Research needed before writing:**
- Confirm Series B investment date and lead investors
- Compile all pre-Series B press coverage into a source document
- Identify what Copia publicly stated about their gross margin and distribution model in 2019-2021
- Confirm the specific factors cited in the shutdown announcement

**Why Sendy:**

## THIRD RETROSPECTIVE CANDIDATE: WAVE SENEGAL (COUNTERCASE)

**Why Wave:**
- Active and growing - the countercase to Copia and Sendy
- Would have flagged regulatory dependency and telco partnership economics heavily at Series A
- The interesting question: what did Wave do that neutralised the flags the tool would have raised?
- JP has direct operator experience in the Francophone West Africa mobile money market - strongest possible operator lens for this case

**Note:** Wave is a positive outcome retrospective. The format changes slightly - instead of "what the tool missed," the question becomes "what did the founders do to navigate what the tool would have flagged?" That is a more sophisticated and arguably more useful analysis for VC readers.
