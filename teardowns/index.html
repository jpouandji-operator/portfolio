Research Database

A research database for people putting money into African companies.

Pick a market, a sector, and the question you are trying to answer. The tool returns what is actually recorded about it: the regulation, the numbers, and what an operator who has worked there would tell you. Where there is no record, it says so.

Who is this for?+
Anyone assessing an African company. Investors and lenders running diligence, founders checking whether a market works the way they assume, analysts who need the regulatory position before a call. You do not need to know the region already. The tool explains what it returns.
What does it give me?+
Three things, always separated so you know which is which: source documents from central banks and regulators, read in full rather than summarised. Verified figures tied to a specific country and sector. And operator observations, things practitioners working inside these markets have said that are written down nowhere else. Every claim shows which record it came from.
What will it refuse to do?+
Invent an answer. If there is no record covering your question, the tool returns nothing and logs the gap rather than producing something plausible. It will also tell you when a claim rests on a single conversation rather than measured data, so you know what weight to put on it.

Comparison Senegal and Rwanda · fintech · reaching customers profitably ← Change the question
The answer
Both markets have made it impossible to lock in customers through partnership deals. They did it in completely different ways.
Senegal

The regional central bank for West Africa, the BCEAO, banned exclusive partnership deals between telecom companies and fintechs in 2024REG-009. A fintech cannot sign a deal that locks a phone network to itself and shuts competitors out.

Rwanda

No ban was needed. Rwanda connected the two main mobile money networks, MTN and Airtel, so money moves freely between themREG-021. Being on one network stopped being an advantage because everyone can reach everyone.

What this means if you are looking at a deal If a company tells you its advantage is an exclusive deal with a phone network, that advantage does not exist in either market. In Senegal the law voided it. In Rwanda the infrastructure made it pointless. What actually defends a business in both places is operational: enough agents holding enough cash in enough places. Harder to build, harder to copy, which is the point.

What is missing
No hard numbers on agent economics in either market. The claim that operational depth is what defends a business comes from practitioner conversations, not measured data. Treat it as a direction to investigate, not a figure for a model. This gap is logged and being worked on.
●
How a figure qualifies. Numbers enter the database only if they pass three checks: published within the last two years or flagged as older; specific to the exact country and sector rather than an Africa-wide average; and interpretable, meaning it can be explained in terms of what it means for deploying capital. All three: stored as a verified figure. Two: stored with a stated caveat. One or none: marked directional and never presented as fact.
●
Coverage, and how it grows. Senegal, Cote d'Ivoire and Cameroun are deepest because I have worked in them and can explain what a rule means in practice, not just what it says. Kenya, Rwanda, Nigeria and Ghana are covered from the official record. Uganda and Tanzania are thin and marked with a dashed outline. The method is deliberately repeatable, so each new market is added the same way rather than requiring a different approach, and the comparisons get sharper with every one.
