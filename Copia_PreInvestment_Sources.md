## PART 1: DEAL CONTEXT - WHAT WAS PUBLICLY KNOWN AT THE TIME

### The Investment Decision Point
**Round assessed:** Series C - $50 million
**Date:** January 19, 2022
**Lead investor:** Goodwell Investments
**Co-investors:** Zebu Investment Partners, U.S. International Development Finance Corporation (DFC), Koa Labs, Lightrock, DEG (Deutsche Investitions- und Entwicklungsgesellschaft), Perivoli Innovations
**Note:** A $5 million DFC tranche had also been disclosed in September 2020 as a pre-Series C bridge.
**Total raised to date at Series C:** Approximately $83.5 million (Series A $2M + $4M early rounds + Series B $26M + DFC bridge $5M + Series C $50M)

### What Copia Said It Was (pre-Series C, from public sources)

**The model as publicly described (Quartz Africa, November 2019; Techpoint, November 2019):**
- B2C e-commerce platform targeting low-to-middle income consumers in rural and peri-urban Kenya
- Agent network model: local shopkeepers serve as ordering points and delivery pickup locations
- Customers walk into agent shops, place orders via the agent, pay cash, collect delivery
- USSD ordering available for feature phone users
- 5,000 agents at Series B (November 2019)
- 450,000 consumers served, 3 million orders fulfilled, operating in central Kenya only at Series B

**The model as publicly described by Series C (January 2022):**
- Expanded to "over 50,000 agents" (claimed in multiple sources by late 2021/early 2022)
- Operations in Kenya and Uganda (Uganda expansion in July 2021)
- "Over 2 million consumers served, 13 million orders fulfilled" (WeeTracker, May 2024 citing pre-shutdown figures going back to 2021/22)
- CEO Tim Steel quote (TechCrunch, referenced in WeeTracker): "annual growth of 100% in recent years"
- Series C use of funds: growing model across East Africa - Kenya and Uganda, Rwanda and Tanzania in future

**What Copia said about its thesis:**
- CEO Tim Steel (Quartz Africa, November 2019): "We built our business on a mobile platform to bring retail services to low to middle income African consumers - consumers who are remote, unbanked, unconnected and who may not have a valid ID or address."
- Differentiation from Jumia: "Other e-commerce players are focused on the top of the pyramid with middle-class and elite."
- Agent rationale: "We established relationships with agents in these areas because those agents are trusted members of the community and through them we build a direct relationship with the consumer."

**What was NOT disclosed publicly before Series C:**
- Gross margin per order
- Agent commission structure or % of revenue paid to agents
- Customer acquisition cost
- Unit economics (LTV, payback period, order frequency)
- Whether the model was profitable at any geography or cohort
- Burn rate or runway

## PART 2: MARKET CONTEXT AT THE TIME OF SERIES C (January 2022)

**Kenya e-commerce context:**
- Jumia had been struggling with unit economics across Africa since 2019 IPO
- Multiple African e-commerce players had shut down or contracted by 2021
- Quartz Africa noted (at Series B, 2019): "questions over the viability of large e-commerce operations across Africa as well as the size of the actual addressable middle class market"
- Konga (Nigeria) had been sold off in 2018 with Naspers and Kinnevik writing off losses
- Kenya was recording strong VC growth: Partech data showed Kenya funding up 33% in 2022

**What was publicly known about rural Kenya e-commerce economics:**
- Last-mile logistics costs in Africa run approximately 8x global benchmarks (FieldAssist data - note: this specific study was 2025, but the structural problem was well understood by 2022)
- Agent-based distribution in African markets: agents typically receive 40-45% of the commission pool (ICTD Working Paper 192 - note: published 2024 but underlying commission structures were operator-known in 2022)
- No primary public data on Copia's gross margin, order economics, or agent commission rates was available before Series C

**The Uganda expansion signal (July 2021 - 6 months before Series C):**
- Copia expanded to Uganda in July 2021
- No unit economics disclosed for Uganda
- The expansion was framed as market growth, not profitability

## PART 3: IC CHECKLIST INPUTS - RECONSTRUCTED FROM PUBLIC INFORMATION

What follows is the IC Checklist run as it would have appeared at Series C (January 2022) using only publicly available information.

### Deal Context Inputs
- **Market:** Kenya (primary) + Uganda (secondary, 6 months old)
- **Sector:** E-commerce / Logistics
- **Stage:** Series C
- **Ticket:** $50M (disclosed)

### Dimension 1: Distribution Economics and Moat

**Gross margin claimed:** Not publicly disclosed. No figure available.
**Distribution model:** Agent network (local shopkeepers as ordering/pickup points) + own delivery infrastructure (warehouses, trucks)
**Stack breakdown:** Not publicly disclosed. Agent commissions not disclosed. Delivery cost structure not disclosed.
**Channel exclusivity/moat:** Semi-exclusive at best. Agents are independent shopkeepers. No contractual exclusivity disclosed. Any well-capitalised competitor could theoretically recruit the same shopkeepers.

**IC Checklist input status:**
- d1_margin: EMPTY - no public disclosure
- d1_model: "Last-mile logistics owned" + "Mobile money agent network" hybrid
- d1_stack: "No - margin stated as a single number" (actually not even stated - no number at all)
- d1_moat: "Semi-exclusive - preferred status, not contractual" (most charitable reading)

**Gate status: RED - insufficient information on distribution economics**

### Dimension 2: Regulatory Timing and Incumbent Capture

**Regulatory dependency:** General business registration + logistics licensing. No fintech licence required for core model.
**Specific risk named:** Not in public sources pre-Series C.
**Incumbent capture risk:** Safaricom (M-Pesa) had financial services ambitions but not a direct e-commerce competitor at the time. Traditional retail/wholesale distributors had no regulatory access to block Copia.
**Contingency:** Not discussed in public sources.

**IC Checklist input status:**
- d2_dep: "Operates under general business registration"
- d2_scenario: "No - not addressed"
- d2_incumbent: "No significant incumbent in this specific niche"
- d2_contingency: "Not asked yet"

**Gate status: AMBER - regulatory risk not discussed but sector has lower regulatory volatility than fintech**

### Dimension 3: Exit Pathway Realism and Acquirability

**Stated exit route:** Not discussed in public sources pre-Series C.
**Named acquirers:** None named publicly.
**Acquirability:** Unknown. No disclosure on governance, cap table structure, audit status.

**IC Checklist input status:**
- d3_route: "Not discussed"
- d3_acquirers: "Not discussed"
- d3_acquirable: "Not assessed"

**Gate status: RED - exit thesis not established in public record**

### Dimension 4: Demand Validation and Recurrence

**Primary demand evidence:** Paying customers at scale - "450,000 consumers, 3M orders" (Series B 2019), "2M+ consumers, 13M orders" (by Series C 2022).
**Recurrence:** "Annual growth of 100%" cited by CEO - but this is revenue/order growth, not retention. No cohort retention data publicly disclosed. No data on % of customers returning without promotions.
**Behaviour replaced and switching cost:** Cash purchases at local duka (small shop). Copia's agent model replicates the duka experience digitally. The switching cost for the customer is low - they can walk into the same agent shop and buy directly instead of ordering through Copia.

**IC Checklist input status:**
- d4_evidence: "Paying customers at scale (1,000+)" - technically passes on volume
- d4_recurrence: "No data on repeat usage yet" - no cohort data in public record
- d4_behaviour: "Founder described the behaviour but not the cost" - duka shopping described, switching cost not quantified

**Gate status: AMBER - paying customers at scale but no recurrence data in public record**

### Dimension 5: Team Resilience and Network Depth

**In-market experience:** Tracey Turner (founder/chair) - previously lived in Kenya, founded MicroPlace (sold to eBay). Jonathan Lewis - social venture background. Tim Steel (CEO) - operating in Kenya. The team had genuine Africa-facing credentials.
**Crisis navigation:** The Uganda expansion (July 2021) and the Kenya focus were operational decisions, but no named crisis navigation was in the public record pre-Series C.
**Network depth:** Not publicly assessed. The investors (Goodwell, DFC, Lightrock) were DFI-adjacent suggesting good institutional relationships. No named regulatory or distribution network relationships in public record.

**IC Checklist input status:**
- d5_experience: "Founder has 2-5 years in this market" (Steel as CEO; Turner as chair with prior Kenya exposure)
- d5_crisis: "Not discussed"
- d5_network: "Not assessed"

**Gate status: AMBER - experience present but network depth not assessable from public record**

### Dimension 6: Currency and FX Risk

**Revenue currency:** Kenyan Shilling (KES) and Ugandan Shilling (UGX) - both local currency
**Cost currency:** Mix - local agent commissions in KES/UGX, but imported goods (significant share of catalogue) priced with USD exposure, technology infrastructure in USD
**Currency regime:** Kenya - managed float with reasonable stability at the time. Uganda - managed float.
**Hedging plan:** Not disclosed.

**IC Checklist input status:**
- d6_currency: "Revenue local currency, some costs in USD or EUR"
- d6_regime: "Managed float with reasonable stability (Kenya, Ghana, Rwanda)"
- d6_hedge: "Partial - aware of the issue, no formal plan"

**Gate status: AMBER - manageable FX exposure but no disclosed hedge strategy**

## PART 4: WHAT THE TOOL WOULD HAVE FLAGGED

**Red dimensions (2):**
1. Distribution economics and moat - no margin data, no stack disclosure, no contractual exclusivity
2. Exit pathway - no acquirer thesis, no governance disclosure

**Amber dimensions (3):**
3. Demand validation - paying customers confirmed but no recurrence data
4. Team resilience - experience present, network depth unassessable
5. Currency/FX - manageable but undisclosed hedge

**Green dimensions (1):**
6. Regulatory - lower volatility sector, no significant incumbent capture risk

**Memo closing flag (as tool would generate):**
"Critical gaps before IC: Distribution economics and moat, Exit pathway realism and acquirability. A founder who cannot answer the questions above with specific numbers, named entities, and documented scenarios has not stress-tested this market. Resolve these before the meeting, not during it."

**IC Questions the tool would have generated for distribution:**
1. "Walk me through the full margin stack from your product to the end customer. Every intermediary, every fee, every commission layer. Give me percentages, not narrative."
2. "What happens to your distribution model if your main channel partner - agent, telco, or distributor - doubles their fee or signs an exclusive with a competitor? Name the specific entity and what your plan B is."
3. "Is your distribution channel contractually exclusive or are you one of several products your agents or partners sell? What stops a well-capitalised competitor from paying your agents 20% more than you do?"

## PART 5: WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED

**Timeline post-Series C (January 2022):**

- **July 2021 (pre-Series C):** Copia expanded to Uganda
- **January 2022:** Series C closes at $50M. Total raised approximately $83.5M.
- **December 2022:** Copia suspended Uganda operations, citing "economic downturn and constrained capital markets." Chose to focus on Kenyan markets. (Source: Techpoint Africa, July 2024)
- **December 2023:** Copia raised a $20M Series C extension round led by Enza Capital, with Goodwell, DFC, and Sorenson Foundation. (Source: WeeTracker, May 2024)
- **July 2023:** Copia cut approximately 700 staff - 25% of Kenyan headcount. (Source: WeeTracker, May 2024)
- **May 16, 2024:** CEO Tim Steel communicated to staff the possibility of "far-reaching organizational restructuring or complete shutdown." Company legally obligated to provide one month's notice of redundancy. (Source: WeeTracker, TechWeez, May 2024)
- **May 17, 2024:** Copia's board approved Chapter 7 bankruptcy filing. Balance sheet at filing: $45.5M in liabilities, $404,000 in assets, $20,300 in cash. $17M owed to WTI Fund Inc. as senior secured creditor. (Source: Launch Base Africa, November 2024)
- **May 2024:** All 1,500 remaining employees laid off. KPMG (Makenzi Muthusi and Julius Ngonga) appointed as administrators.
- **July 2024:** Kenyan unit liquidation confirmed. KPMG failed to secure new investment. (Source: Techpoint Africa, Launch Base Africa)
- **Total raised at collapse:** Approximately $116-123M (sources vary slightly). Cash at collapse: $20,300.

**The structural failure modes documented post-collapse:**

1. **Capital dependency:** The company's reliance on external funding exposed a vulnerability: a model heavily dependent on constant capital infusions to sustain operations. When fresh funding dried up, the business unraveled rapidly.

2. **Unit economics never proven:** The $20,300 cash against $45.5M liabilities at bankruptcy filing indicates that despite 13M+ orders and $120M+ raised, the model never reached cash generation.

3. **Uganda withdrawal as early signal:** The Uganda suspension in December 2022 - just 11 months after the Series C closed - was publicly framed as capital market constraints. But the speed of the withdrawal suggests the Uganda unit economics were worse than the Kenya model, and Kenya itself was not profitable.

4. **Agent network scale did not produce unit economics improvement:** Growth from 5,000 agents (Series B) to 50,000 agents (Series C) did not produce the expected distribution cost efficiency. The agent commission structure and last-mile delivery costs appear to have remained structurally challenging at scale.

## PART 6: WHAT THIS TEACHES THE MODEL

**Where the tool would have flagged correctly:**

The RED flag on distribution economics was the right call. The tool would have asked: "Walk me through the full margin stack from your product to the end customer." That question - if answered honestly by the Copia team in January 2022 - would have surfaced that 11 months later they would be withdrawing from Uganda and that the agent commission + last-mile delivery cost structure was not producing profitable unit economics at scale.

The RED flag on exit pathway was also correct. With $120M+ raised and a governance structure that included multiple DFI investors, the cap table and exit pathway complexity should have been assessed before another $50M went in.

**Where the tool missed something material:**

The tool does not assess capital efficiency trajectory - how much capital is required per dollar of growth. Copia's 100% annual growth figure cited by the CEO obscured that the capital required to produce that growth was itself growing. A VC looking at this deal in January 2022 would have seen: $83.5M raised to date, 100% annual growth. What the tool cannot surface is whether that growth is compounding margin improvement or compounding cash burn. That is the Unit Economics Trajectory dimension - currently flagged as a missing dimension in the IC Checklist output, but not yet built.

The tool also does not assess the distinction between demand validation by volume and demand validation by willingness to pay without subsidy. Copia's agent model required the company to maintain the delivery infrastructure. The question "do customers return without a promotion or founder intervention?" is in the tool - but "do customers return without the company subsidising the delivery cost?" is a more precise version that this model specifically required.

**Benchmark repository additions from this research:**

- The $20,300 cash against $45.5M liabilities figure is a useful data point for the "model origin / capital efficiency" discussion in war games retrospectives
- The Uganda withdrawal within 11 months of Series C is a documented expansion failure mode - geographic expansion before unit economics proven in primary market
- The agent network scale (5,000 to 50,000) without corresponding unit economics improvement documents that agent network size is not itself a moat

**IC Checklist improvements flagged:**

1. Add a question in Dimension 4 (demand validation): "Does your model require the company to subsidise the customer's cost of access (delivery, logistics, connectivity)? If the subsidy is removed, does the demand hold?"
2. The Unit Economics Trajectory missing dimension panel should specifically flag the capital efficiency question for e-commerce and logistics models.
